Modeling Melodic Dictation

Chapter 3 Individual Differences

3.0.1 Intro/Rationale

The first two steps of Gary Karpinski’s model of melodic dictation (Karpinski 2000, 1990) rely exclusively on the mental representation of melodic information. Karpinski conceptualizes the first stage of hearing as solely involving the physical motions on the tympanic membrane as well as the listener’s attention to the musical stimulus. This stage is distinguished from that of short-term melodic memory which refers to the amount of melodic information that can be represented in conscious awareness. Given that neither stage of the first two steps of Karpinski’s model requires any sort of musical expertise, every individual with normal hearing should be able to partake in the first two steps of the process.9 It is only with explicit education in music theory and aural skills when individuals learn musical literacy do people develop the ability to start to link what is heard to what can then be understood and then notated. Additionally, with more musical training, individuals also increase in their ability to perform musical tasks CITES. Though attempting to investigate all parts of this process from hearing to short-term melodic memory to musical understanding to notation is not only cumbersome from a theoretical perspective, but parsing out what happens at each stage in a dictator’s mind is unfeasible practically due to the amount of variables at play. In order to get a clearer picture of what mechanisms are involved in this process, the steps must be be investigated in turn. This chapter investigates the first two steps of the Karpinski model by investigating individual factors that contribute to how someone learns a melody that do not depend on their knowledge of Western musical notation. By understanding which, if any, individual factors play a role in this process, it will inform what can be reasonably expected of individuals when other musical variables are introduced.
